1. The Enduring Challenge of Gravitational Theory
Since its inception, gravitational theory has evolved through successive revolutionary steps. Isaac Newton's law of universal gravitation provided a remarkably accurate description of astronomical dynamics, framing gravity as a force proportional to the product of masses (m_1 \times m_2). Albert Einstein's General Relativity (GR) superseded this by recasting gravity as the curvature of spacetime geometry, brilliantly solving the problem of action-at-a-distance and providing the current definitive classical theory.
However, the geometric elegance of GR exists in stark contradiction with the principles of quantum mechanics and the successful description of non-gravitational forces through quantum field theories (the Standard Model). The pursuit of a quantum theory of gravity, a problem that has persisted for nearly a century, reveals fundamental incompatibilities at the Planck scale.
2. Crises in Modern Physics and the Need for a New Foundation
The current state of physics presents two critical crises that necessitate a radical departure from established frameworks:
 * The Unification Crisis: Gravity remains algebraically isolated from the three quantum forces (electromagnetic, weak, and strong). Existing attempts often rely on high-dimensional theories, yet lack a single, geometric principle that ties all fundamental interactions into one coherent structure.
 * The Measurement and Spacetime Crisis: The quantum measurement problem is unresolved, and the fundamental nature of spacetime at the Planck scale is unknown. Modern theoretical insights strongly suggest that spacetime itself may be an emergent phenomenon, built from underlying quantum information structures, specifically entanglement.
